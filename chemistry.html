Plastics are polymers made from monomers. The monomers join together, often end-to-end.

Sources of Microplastics

  • Sewage, beach litter, storm runoff of plastic dust from street and roads, car tires, accidental spills from industry, ocean-dumping from ships, and the agriculture industry
    • Primary microplastics are like microbeads and fibers which can pass through sewage system plants because it can not be filtered
    • Secondary microplastics are fragmented pieces of plastics that are broken down by different acts of nature, ending up in the ocean or garbage piles if not cleaned up

The Chemistry of Microplastics (Reactions)

Primary Microplastics (plasticizers are used for primary as well)

Created through air-blast technology (microbeads).

Blasting acrylic, melamine, or polyester microplastic scrubbers at machinery, engines, and boat hulls to remove rust and paint.

They become contaminated with heavy metals such as cadmium, chromium, and lead during the process occasionally.

These plasticizers alter the properties of the plastics because it is an additive that either increases the plasticity or decreases the viscosity of a material.

Plasticizers embed themselves between the chains of polymers and space them apart, therefore increasing the free volume and lowering the glass transition temperature for the plastic and making it softer (this does not apply to all the effects of plasticization)

This concept of lowering the cold flex temperature allows for the plastic to improve its flexibility and durability (plastic toy to water bottles). For example, polyvinyl chloride (PVC), a microplastic, uses phthalate esters as plasticizers. (90% of plasticizers are used in PVC)

Secondary Microplastics

At large garbage patches, chemicals called “plasticizers” go into the water as well Plastic undergoes physical, chemical and biological weathering, decreasing its size through fragmentation and altering its original shape, chemical composition and surface characteristics. This occurs when they enter the environment

When we pollute the oceans with disposable items, the plastics are not completely destroyed yet, so they end up in landfills or sewers. Plastic is less dense than water, so it floats on water.
